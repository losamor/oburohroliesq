International law was created with a promise. That promise was simple: no matter how powerful a country became, there would still exist rules that restrained violence, protected civilians, respected sovereignty, and preserved human dignity. After the horrors of the Second World War, after the Holocaust, after the collapse of Europe into industrial-scale destruction, the world decided that humanity could not continue operating solely on power politics. It needed rules.



The United Nations Charter was adopted in 1945. The Geneva Conventions were strengthened. The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) was later created. International humanitarian law developed further. Refugee conventions emerged. Human rights courts were established. International criminal tribunals were formed.



The idea sounded noble.



But decades later, many people across the world have started asking a deeply uncomfortable question: if international law truly exists, why does it appear to restrain weak countries more than powerful ones?



Why do some states face sanctions, military pressure, investigations, and condemnation for violations, while others appear able to operate with near-complete impunity?



Why does international law seem rigid when ordinary countries attempt to defend borders, manage migration, or exercise sovereignty, yet appear strangely flexible when major global powers or their allies are involved?



And most importantly: if civilians continue dying in massive numbers while international institutions merely release statements of concern, what exactly is international law protecting?



These questions became impossible to ignore during the Israel–Gaza war.



Israel, Gaza, and the Collapse of Moral Credibility



The modern phase of the Israel–Gaza conflict escalated dramatically after the October 7, 2023 Hamas attacks on Israel, during which approximately 1,200 people were killed and hundreds were taken hostage, according to Israeli authorities. Israel responded with a large-scale military campaign in Gaza.



The human consequences were catastrophic.



By 2025 and 2026, multiple studies suggested that the official death tolls in Gaza likely understated the true scale of deaths caused directly and indirectly by the war. Peer-reviewed studies published in medical journals such as The Lancet and discussed in The BMJ estimated that violent deaths alone may have exceeded 75,000 during the first sixteen months of the conflict, significantly higher than the figures initially reported by Gaza authorities. Other analyses suggested the broader death toll, including indirect deaths caused by disease, starvation, infrastructure collapse, and lack of medical care, could eventually become dramatically higher. ([theguardian.com](http://theguardian.com/))



What made the situation even more disturbing was the sheer scale of destruction. Entire residential areas were flattened. Hospitals were damaged or destroyed. Schools, roads, refugee camps, and public infrastructure collapsed under sustained bombardment. Millions of civilians were displaced.



The United Nations repeatedly warned of humanitarian catastrophe.



And yet, despite the scale of suffering, international law appeared unable—or unwilling—to stop the devastation.



That failure severely damaged global confidence in the credibility of international institutions.



To many observers around the world, especially in developing countries, the message seemed obvious: international law is enforced selectively.



Supporters of Israel argued that Israel had a right to defend itself after the October 7 attacks. That is true under international law. States possess a recognized right to self-defense.



But international humanitarian law also imposes limits.



The Geneva Conventions require distinction between combatants and civilians. They prohibit disproportionate force. They prohibit collective punishment. They require protection of civilian populations even during war.



Critics of Israel argued that the scale of civilian destruction, displacement, starvation, and infrastructural collapse went beyond legitimate military operations and entered the territory of grave violations of international humanitarian law.



Some governments, human rights organizations, legal scholars, and international observers even described aspects of the campaign as potentially constituting war crimes or crimes against humanity. South Africa filed a genocide case against Israel before the International Court of Justice.



Whether one agrees fully with those accusations or not, one fact remains undeniable: the international system failed to produce swift accountability.



There were resolutions.



There were statements.



There were appeals for restraint.



But there was no decisive enforcement.



For many people, especially outside the Western world, this reinforced the belief that international law often functions more as a political instrument than as a universally applied legal system.



The Historical Roots of the Israel–Palestine Conflict



Understanding the emotional intensity of the Israel–Palestine conflict requires understanding the long history behind it.



Historically, the land known today as Israel and Palestine has been inhabited by different peoples for thousands of years. In ancient times, Jewish kingdoms existed in the region. The Jewish people maintained religious, cultural, and historical ties to the land for centuries.



The area later came under Roman control. Following Jewish revolts against Roman rule—especially the Bar Kokhba revolt in the second century CE—many Jews were killed, displaced, or scattered across different parts of the world. The Romans renamed the area Syria Palaestina, partly in an attempt to weaken Jewish identification with the territory.



Over centuries, the region came under Byzantine, Islamic, Crusader, Ottoman, and eventually British control.



By the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, modern Zionism emerged as a political movement advocating for a Jewish homeland, especially amid growing antisemitism in Europe.



Following the Holocaust, international support for the establishment of a Jewish state increased dramatically.



In 1947, the United Nations proposed partitioning British-controlled Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states. Jewish leaders accepted the proposal; many Arab leaders rejected it.



In 1948, Israel declared independence.



War followed immediately.



Hundreds of thousands of Palestinians were displaced during the conflict—a tragedy Palestinians refer to as the Nakba, meaning “catastrophe.”



Since then, the region has experienced repeated wars, uprisings, occupations, peace negotiations, failed agreements, settlement expansion, terrorism, rocket attacks, military operations, and deepening mistrust.



The result is one of the most emotionally charged and politically divisive conflicts in modern history.



But historical complexity does not erase legal obligations.



Even during war, international humanitarian law is supposed to protect civilians.



That is the entire point of international law.



Yet the Gaza war convinced millions of people that the international legal system either lacks the power or lacks the willingness to restrain powerful actors.



International Law and Selective Enforcement



One of the greatest criticisms of international law today is inconsistency.



Smaller countries are often pressured aggressively for violations.



Powerful countries and their allies are treated differently.



This perception did not emerge only from Gaza.



The United States invasion of Iraq in 2003 remains one of the clearest examples. The invasion was justified partly on claims regarding weapons of mass destruction that were never found. Critics argued the invasion violated international law because it lacked proper United Nations authorization.



Yet despite the immense destruction and instability that followed, no major Western leader faced meaningful international legal consequences.



Similarly, when Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022, the international community responded with massive sanctions, diplomatic isolation, and extensive condemnation.



But many countries in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East pointed out what they viewed as inconsistency: some wars trigger extraordinary outrage while others produce diplomatic caution.



That perception damages the legitimacy of the entire international system.



Laws lose moral authority when people believe they are applied selectively.



International Humanitarian Law and Sovereignty



International humanitarian law (IHL) was designed to regulate armed conflict.



It governs how wars are fought.



It protects civilians, prisoners of war, and noncombatants.



It restricts targeting methods.



It also recognizes sovereignty.



Under the United Nations Charter, states generally cannot use force against another sovereign state except under limited conditions, including self-defense or authorization from the UN Security Council.



This principle is central to modern international order.



And yet, global politics frequently complicates its application.



For example, counterterrorism operations across borders have often created controversy. Powerful countries sometimes conduct military strikes in foreign territories against terrorist groups, arguing that the host state is unable or unwilling to address the threat.



Supporters argue such actions are necessary for security.



Critics argue they undermine sovereignty and weaken international legal norms.



This tension reveals another core problem: international law frequently collides with geopolitical reality.



Powerful states possess military capabilities that weaker states cannot realistically challenge.



As a result, international law sometimes appears dependent not on legal principle, but on political leverage.



The United Kingdom, Immigration, and the ECHR Debate



While international law appears weak in preventing wars, many critics argue it becomes extremely powerful in domestic governance—particularly regarding immigration and human rights litigation.



The United Kingdom has become one of the clearest examples of this debate.



The modern European human rights system emerged after World War II. The European Convention on Human Rights was drafted largely in response to the atrocities of fascism, genocide, and authoritarianism.



The Convention established fundamental rights, including protection against torture, arbitrary detention, and unjust state interference.



The United Kingdom was one of the founding signatories.



Over time, the Convention became incorporated into British law through measures such as the Human Rights Act 1998.



Many legal protections now influence British immigration policy, asylum law, criminal justice, and deportation procedures.



Two articles have become especially controversial.



Article 3



Article 3 prohibits torture and inhuman or degrading treatment.



This protection is considered absolute.



Courts have often ruled that individuals cannot be deported to countries where they face serious risks of torture, persecution, or degrading treatment.



Article 8



Article 8 protects the right to private and family life.



This provision has frequently been invoked in deportation appeals.



Critics argue that foreign offenders sometimes avoid deportation by citing family relationships established in the United Kingdom.



Supporters argue these protections are necessary safeguards against state abuse.



The debate has become increasingly heated because migration pressures have intensified across Europe.



The UK asylum system has faced severe strain in recent years. Government statistics showed over 100,000 asylum claims annually by 2025, while large numbers of asylum seekers remained housed in temporary accommodation funded by taxpayers. ([gov.uk](http://gov.uk/))



Many British politicians argue that parts of the human rights framework are now being exploited.



Critics claim some individuals enter legally on work or student visas and later seek asylum as a strategy to remain permanently.



Others argue the courts have repeatedly limited the government’s ability to deport dangerous individuals.



Cases involving terrorism suspects, foreign criminals, and immigration detainees have intensified public frustration.



One of the most famous examples involved indefinite detention laws introduced after the September 11 attacks. In the Belmarsh case, the UK’s highest court ruled that indefinite detention without trial violated human rights principles.



To supporters of civil liberties, the ruling protected fundamental justice.



To critics, it showed how human rights law could restrict national security policy.



Similarly, debates over prisoner voting rights, deportation appeals, and asylum removals have fueled growing political demands for reform.



By 2026, even mainstream European governments were openly discussing reinterpretation of the ECHR framework to allow stricter immigration enforcement. ([theguardian.com](http://theguardian.com/))



This reflects a broader global pattern.



Many governments increasingly believe that international legal frameworks impose obligations on democratic states while offering insufficient flexibility to address modern migration crises.



Sovereignty Versus Universal Rights



At the center of this debate lies a profound philosophical question.



Who ultimately decides the identity, security, and immigration policies of a nation-state?



The state itself?



Or international legal institutions?



Supporters of strong international human rights protections argue that history proves governments cannot always be trusted to protect minorities, refugees, or vulnerable populations.



Without supranational legal oversight, they argue, states may abuse power.



Critics respond differently.



They argue that modern international law increasingly overrides democratic self-determination.



Citizens elect governments partly to control borders, manage migration, and preserve public order.



If courts or international treaties continually override those policies, critics ask whether national sovereignty still exists in a meaningful sense.



This tension has become politically explosive across Europe.



Nigeria, Governance, and International Double Standards



Nigeria presents another perspective on the failures and contradictions of international law.



Nigeria faces severe security challenges, including terrorism, kidnapping, banditry, corruption, separatist agitation, and economic instability.



But many Nigerians also feel international actors misunderstand the internal realities of the country.



International narratives often reduce Nigeria’s security crisis to simplistic headlines about Boko Haram, terrorism, or instability.



Yet many Nigerians argue the situation is far more complicated.



They point to corruption, political sponsorship networks, governance failures, elite manipulation, economic desperation, and weak institutions as deeper drivers of insecurity.



Some also argue Nigeria’s governance structure itself remains fundamentally unstable.



Nigeria is an extraordinarily diverse country with hundreds of ethnic groups, religious divisions, colonial-era boundaries, and long-standing regional tensions.



Since independence, debates over federalism, restructuring, resource control, and national identity have remained unresolved.



Separatist movements such as Biafra agitation reflect ongoing dissatisfaction among segments of the population.



Many Nigerians therefore argue that governance structures imported from other political systems do not fully align with Nigeria’s historical realities or social complexity.



Against this background, international intervention becomes highly sensitive.



Questions about sovereignty become unavoidable.



Many Nigerians object strongly to foreign governments appearing to define Nigeria primarily through security problems.



They argue that foreign governments cannot fully understand realities on the ground simply through intelligence reports, media coverage, or diplomatic briefings.



This frustration increases when powerful countries appear willing to intervene militarily, politically, or economically without fully understanding the internal dynamics of the society involved.



Critics argue that international law should protect weaker states from external interference just as vigorously as it protects individual human rights.



Yet many believe global institutions rarely challenge powerful states decisively.



That perception again fuels distrust.



The United States and Immigration Sovereignty



Immigration debates in the United States raise another important issue.



Every sovereign state possesses the legal authority to control entry into its territory.



States issue visas at their discretion.



They can deny admission.



They can deport non-citizens under domestic law.



This principle is widely recognized internationally.



In recent years, the United States adopted stricter immigration enforcement policies under different administrations.



These measures triggered enormous political controversy.



Critics described them as harsh, discriminatory, or inhumane.



Supporters argued they represented legitimate exercises of sovereignty.



At the heart of the debate lies a difficult reality: international law does not grant individuals an unrestricted right to enter or remain in another country.



Refugee protections exist.



Human rights protections exist.



But sovereign states still maintain broad powers over immigration policy.



Many people across the world increasingly support this principle.



They believe countries should retain the right to decide who enters, who remains, and under what conditions.



This sentiment has strengthened partly because of growing public frustration over irregular migration, border security concerns, economic pressure, and integration challenges.



Why Public Trust in International Law Is Declining



The crisis facing international law today is not simply legal.



It is moral.



People no longer believe the system operates fairly.



When civilians die in huge numbers during wars and accountability appears absent, trust collapses.



When courts prevent deportations but appear powerless against mass violence, frustration grows.



When powerful states act unilaterally while weaker states are lectured about legal compliance, accusations of hypocrisy intensify.



International law depends heavily on legitimacy.



Unlike domestic legal systems, there is no single global police force capable of enforcing rules equally against all nations.



International law functions largely through consent, diplomacy, treaties, economic pressure, alliances, and political will.



That system only works if states and populations believe the rules are broadly fair.



Once that belief weakens, the authority of international law weakens with it.



The Fundamental Contradiction



The modern international legal order suffers from a deep contradiction.



On one hand, it seeks universal human rights.



On the other hand, the world remains organized around sovereign nation-states with unequal power.



Powerful countries possess stronger militaries, larger economies, greater diplomatic leverage, and veto powers within institutions like the UN Security Council.



This means enforcement is inherently unequal.



International law can condemn.



It can investigate.



It can issue warrants.



But it often struggles to compel compliance from major powers.



As a result, critics argue that international law frequently restrains middle powers and weaker states more effectively than dominant geopolitical actors.



That perception may be unfair in some cases.



But perceptions matter.



And globally, skepticism is growing.



Does the World Still Need International Law?



Despite all these failures, abolishing international law entirely would create enormous dangers.



Without international humanitarian law, there would be even fewer restrictions on warfare.



Without refugee law, displaced populations would possess even fewer protections.



Without human rights conventions, authoritarian governments could operate with even less restraint.



Without treaties, diplomacy would increasingly depend purely on military and economic power.



The real issue may therefore not be whether international law should exist.



The real issue is whether it can be reformed into something more consistent, credible, and enforceable.



Many critics believe reforms are urgently necessary.



They argue that international institutions must apply standards more equally.



They argue that civilian protection during war must become genuinely enforceable rather than symbolic.



They argue that immigration and asylum systems require modernization to balance humanitarian obligations with state sovereignty.



They argue that international courts should not appear politically selective.



And they argue that powerful countries must be held to the same standards imposed on weaker states.



Without such reforms, distrust will continue growing.



Conclusion



The post-World War II international legal order was built on hope.



It was built on the belief that humanity could rise above raw power politics.



It was built on the promise that civilians would never again be abandoned during mass violence.



But recent decades—and especially recent conflicts—have shaken that belief.



The Israel–Gaza war exposed the limits of international enforcement.



Migration disputes in Europe exposed tensions between human rights and sovereignty.



Global reactions to wars and interventions exposed accusations of double standards.



And throughout the world, growing numbers of people have begun questioning whether international law protects principles—or merely reflects geopolitical power.



That question cannot simply be dismissed.



Because when ordinary people stop believing laws are fair, the legitimacy of the system itself begins to erode.



International law still matters.



But unless it becomes more consistent, more balanced, and more credible, the world may increasingly see it not as a shield for humanity, but as a system that protects some people far more effectively than others.



And that may become its greatest crisis of all.
