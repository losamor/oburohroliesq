**Beyond the “Isms”: Tribalism, Capitalism, Nepotism, Racism, and the Crisis of Accountability in Nigeria**

Nigeria, like much of the modern world, is trapped in arguments about “isms.” Nepotism. Capitalism. Tribalism. Racism. These words are thrown around in outrage, often without discipline or reflection. They have become emotional weapons rather than analytical tools. Yet if Nigeria is to move forward, it must examine these concepts seriously—without sentimentality, without denial, and without selective outrage.

The central problem is not that these forces exist. Every society contains them in some form. The problem is how Nigerians interpret them, misapply them, and often focus on the wrong enemy.

**Tribalism and the Undermining of National Framework**

Nigeria’s history cannot be discussed without acknowledging the Nigerian Civil War (1967–1970), often associated with the secessionist attempt to form Biafra. The war followed political breakdowns, regional distrust, and ethnic violence. But beyond emotion lies a constitutional reality: a nation governed by a supreme constitution cannot permit unilateral secession outside established legal processes.

The argument is not simply about sympathy or grievance. It is about framework. When a group declares independence, creates a flag, anthem, and currency without constitutional authorization or internationally recognized legal mechanisms, it challenges the sovereignty of the state. Whether grievances were valid or not, bypassing legal structures transforms political disagreement into constitutional crisis.

Even today, agitation for ethnic separation often ignores a fundamental question: Is there verified, democratic consensus? Where is the ballot? Where is the plebiscite recognized by national and international institutions? Without lawful procedure, agitation becomes the voice of a faction, not necessarily the will of a majority.

Meanwhile, states such as Lagos were not physically devastated during the war. Economic life continued in parts of the country even as conflict raged elsewhere. This uneven experience of conflict still shapes regional perceptions today.

**Tribalism in Everyday Life: North and South**

In contemporary Nigeria, tribalism is most visible in the mutual suspicion between North and South.

Many southerners perceive northerners as uneducated, economically disadvantaged, or culturally rigid. In southern cities, northern migrants are often seen performing informal labor: scrap collection, street vending, manual cleaning, ice cream hawking, petty trading. They are stereotyped as poor, lazy, or criminally inclined.

Yet such stereotypes ignore structural realities. Informal networks in the South often absorb uneducated southerners into apprenticeship systems—mechanics, tailoring, electronics repair, carpentry—through family and social connections. Northerners arriving without those networks frequently lack the same access. Exclusion then reinforces perception.

At the same time, northerners are frequently associated in national discourse with insurgency, banditry, or extremist violence. Yet crime is not geographically exclusive. Kidnappings, organ trafficking, ritual killings, armed robbery, and cybercrime are prevalent in southern states as well. Victims and perpetrators often belong to the same ethnic groups.

Crime statistics are rarely discussed comparatively or proportionally. Media emphasis can amplify particular atrocities while normalizing others. Many national media operations are headquartered in Abuja or maintain strong southern institutional ties, which inevitably influences narrative flow. This does not mean northern crimes are fabricated; it means perception is shaped by visibility and emphasis.

The result is a cycle: northerners are branded violent; southerners are branded corrupt or fraudulent. Each region highlights the worst of the other while minimizing its own failures.

**Tribalism thrives on selective memory.**

**Education and Northern Self-Sufficiency**

A persistent narrative claims that the North is structurally incapable of self-sustenance. Yet significant private and political investment in northern education exists. Government-approved textbooks are distributed annually to public schools in many northern states, and wealthy political patrons have funded free schooling initiatives.

The North also remains central to agricultural production—supplying grains, tomatoes, and livestock that feed the country. The idea that one region “produces nothing” collapses under economic scrutiny.

If there were ever renewed constitutional debates about autonomy or restructuring, assumptions that one region would automatically dominate or collapse ignore political complexity. Power in Nigeria is layered—economic, agricultural, political, and institutional.

Media, Perception, and the Politics of Image

National narratives often originate from centralized media systems. Atrocities in the North—mass kidnappings, insurgent attacks—receive global headlines. Meanwhile, daily disappearances, ritual killings, and organized crime in southern urban centers may feel normalized locally.

The question is not denial of violence. The question is proportional representation and verification. Exaggeration, sensationalism, or selective amplification distort national self-understanding.

Painting an entire region as irredeemable does not strengthen another region. It deepens division.

**Capitalism and the Oligarchic Realit**y

If there is a structural crisis more urgent than tribal rhetoric, it lies in the concentration of economic power.

Nigeria claims to operate a capitalist system. In practice, it often resembles oligarchy: immense wealth concentrated in the hands of a small circle of politically connected elites. Industrial magnates such as Aliko Dangote and financial figures like Tony Elumelu symbolize both national pride and national controversy.

Large-scale industrialization, banking expansion, refinery construction, and philanthropy coexist with allegations of monopolistic dominance, preferential access to foreign exchange, regulatory capture, and political proximity. When private individuals influence public policy without transparent accountability, capitalism becomes extractive rather than developmental.

Youth unemployment remains high. Small businesses struggle with credit access. Infrastructure deficits persist. Yet public anger is often redirected toward tribal rivalry instead of structural economic reform.

The real question is not whether wealth should exist. It is whether concentrated wealth operates within transparent, competitive, accountable systems.

**Nepotism, Connection, and Social Capita**l

Nigeria’s debate around nepotism differs from Western discourse. In developed contexts, “nepotism babies” are often criticized as beneficiaries of inherited privilege. In Nigeria, many lack access to such privilege altogether.

Instead, connection becomes survival currency. Employment frequently depends on informal networks: a university friend, an uncle, a patron, a religious contact, even transactional relationships. This system is not celebrated as ideology; it is adopted as necessity in weak institutional environments.

The issue is not moral condemnation of individuals using available networks. The issue is institutional fragility that makes connection more powerful than competence.

**Nigerians Abroad: Image, Crime, and Responsibility**

Globalization has made Nigeria’s reputation portable. Some Nigerians abroad have engaged in cyber fraud, drug trafficking, identity theft, and organized crime. These cases damage national image and reinforce stereotypes.

However, criminality is not uniquely Nigerian. Every diaspora community contains both offenders and achievers. Nigerians abroad also include doctors, engineers, professors, nurses, entrepreneurs, and researchers whose contributions rarely trend online.

Racism undeniably exists in parts of Europe and North America. Discrimination in housing, employment, and policing has been documented. But accountability must coexist with this acknowledgment. Not every enforcement of rules constitutes racism. Not every confrontation is prejudice.

Victimhood narratives can sometimes overshadow personal responsibility. At the same time, blanket condemnation of Nigerians abroad ignores systemic bias.

 **The Misplaced Focus**

In Nigeria, public discourse often oscillates between accusing the North of tribalism and accusing the West of racism. Meanwhile, the concentration of economic and political power deepens quietly.

If tribalism declines through education and intermarriage—as trends suggest—it will be because younger generations recognize its futility. But economic concentration and institutional weakness will not dissolve through cultural evolution alone.

A distracted population is easier to govern without accountability.

**Conclusion: The Real Enemy**

Nigeria’s challenge is not merely tribalism. Nor is it simply racism. Nor even nepotism in its colloquial sense.

The deeper crisis is structural:

1. Weak institutions

2. Concentrated economic power

3. Regulatory opacity

4. Elite impunity

5. Distracted public discourse

Tribal hostility harms unity. Racism abroad harms dignity. Nepotistic systems harm merit. But unaccountable concentrations of power harm everyone.

Nigeria will move forward not by amplifying outrage, but by strengthening institutions, enforcing transparency, demanding economic fairness, and cultivating disciplined civic responsibility.

The problem is not the existence of “isms.”

The problem is misunderstanding which ones truly govern us—and failing to confront them where they actually operate.
